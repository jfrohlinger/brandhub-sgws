The Industry Problem

Small Brands. Big Promises.
Short Runways.

Every distributor knows the story. A passionate founder, a promising liquid, an ambitious pitch — and six months later, dead inventory and an unanswered phone.

Small and emerging brands are the lifeblood of portfolio differentiation. Distributors like Southern Glazer's take them on with the best intentions — diversifying the book, supporting the craft movement, answering supplier demand.

But the reality is that most small suppliers are chronically undercapitalized. They need distributor purchases to make payroll. They need the credibility of distribution to raise their next round. They're playing a short game while asking distributors to make a long-term commitment.

The result? Distributors bear 100% of the operational burden while the brand extracts the capital benefit — then struggles to execute the market support that justifies the listing.

The Small Brand Tax

Distributors effectively subsidize undercapitalized brands — providing distribution, warehousing, sales execution, and back-office support in exchange for volume that rarely materializes at the level promised. The industry accepts this as the cost of portfolio diversification. It doesn't have to be.

The Reframe

What if taking a brand on required proof they could fund the market?

Southern already uses its distribution agreement to set terms around pricing, exclusivity, performance windows, and minimum volumes. Why not add a trade marketing commitment?

Requiring a minimum annual trade marketing spend — $25K per state — as a condition of the distribution agreement does several things at once: it validates that the brand has real capital behind it, guarantees local market activation, and shifts the burden of POS execution off your TDMs entirely.

This isn't punitive — it's alignment. A brand that can't commit $25K/state to its own market doesn't have the infrastructure to be a viable long-term partner. Better to know that before you cut the first purchase order.

The Mechanics

End-to-End, Zero Friction

From distribution agreement to in-market execution — how the full cycle runs on autopilot.

1
Distribution Agreement Includes Trade Commitment
When Southern onboards a new small brand, the distribution agreement includes a mandatory annual trade marketing spend — structured as $25,000 per active state. Funds are committed at signing and held in the platform. This is a condition of distribution, not a request.
Legal / Commercial
2
Brand Accesses the brandhub Portal
Each brand gets access to a white-labeled portal under Southern's platform. They can view their committed budget, select from Southern's pre-approved market starter kits, add brand-specific customizations, and submit orders — all within a system Southern controls and brandhub powers.
Platform Access
3
Orders Pool Across the Portfolio
Individual brand orders are aggregated by brandhub into consolidated production runs. Select's production team executes all POS, signage, displays, and materials at pooled volume pricing — significantly better than any single small brand could achieve independently, often exceeding domestic sourcing benchmarks.
Production Pooling
4
Southern Bills the Spread
The price brands see in the portal includes Southern's margin — a defined markup above the pooled production cost. This spread is programmatic revenue: it can be treated as profit, reinvested in market activation funds, or both. brandhub's API connects directly to Southern's billing and ERP systems.
Revenue Generation
5
Automated Bill-Backs & Reconciliation
brandhub's PO automation handles bill-back reconciliation automatically. What currently requires manual back-office entry per brand is reduced to a single data feed into Southern's systems. No manual POS buy entry. No fragmented supplier follow-up. No reconciliation headaches.
Full Automation
